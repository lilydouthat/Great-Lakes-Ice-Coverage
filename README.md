Overview

This project explores historical ice coverage patterns across the Great Lakes and investigates how ice extent has changed over time. Using publicly available environmental and climate datasets, the analysis examines seasonal variability, long-term trends, and the potential relationship between changing ice coverage and broader climate conditions.

The Great Lakes contain approximately 20% of the world’s surface freshwater, making them an important environmental system to study. Ice coverage influences shipping, ecosystems, water levels, recreation, and regional weather patterns throughout the Great Lakes basin.

⸻

Research Questions

* How has Great Lakes ice coverage changed over time?
* Which lakes experience the greatest variability in ice extent?
* Are there observable long-term trends in ice coverage?
* How do severe and mild winter seasons compare?
* What environmental factors may contribute to changes in ice extent?

⸻

Objectives

* Analyze historical Great Lakes ice coverage data.
* Visualize seasonal and annual trends.
* Identify years with unusually high or low ice coverage.
* Explore potential indicators of climate variability.
* Communicate findings through clear visualizations and data storytelling.

Methods

The project includes:

* Data cleaning and preparation
* Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)
* Time-series visualization
* Trend analysis
* Comparative analysis across lakes and years

⸻

Key Findings

* Great Lakes ice coverage has generally decreased since the early 1970s.  
* Despite significant seasonal and year-to-year variability, statistical models identified a persistent long-term downward trend.  
* Results support concerns that changing climate conditions may be influencing winter ice formation across the Great Lakes.

Areas of investigation include:

* Long-term changes in average ice coverage
* Frequency of extreme ice years
* Differences between individual Great Lakes
* Climate-related trends and variability
